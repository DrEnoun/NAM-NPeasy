Introduction

What is Network Pharmacology?

Network pharmacology is a systems-level computational approach that maps multi-compound, multi-target, multi-pathway interactions simultaneously. It answers the question: how do multiple compounds in a natural product work together to produce a biological effect?

🎯
Multi-target mapping
Identifies all proteins your compounds are predicted to engage — not just one target, but the whole network.
🌿
Polypharmacology explanation
Explains why polyherbal formulations work better than isolated compounds — different compounds engage different targets.
🔬
Hypothesis generation
Predicts which targets and pathways to validate experimentally, guiding wet lab work rationally.
📋
Regulatory documentation
Provides mechanistic evidence for regulatory submissions — the "why" that transforms efficacy data into a mechanism claim.
When NOT to use network pharmacology alone: NP is a hypothesis-generating tool, not a proof of mechanism. Computational predictions must always be validated — by existing wet lab data, molecular docking, or new experiments. Never publish NP results without a validation layer.
Before You Start

Decision Tree — Which workflow applies to you?

Your starting point determines which phases are required. Use this decision tree before beginning any NP study.

🌿 Starting point: your compound set
Q1 — What is the status of your compounds?
→ Unknown herbal extract, no prior characterisation — Start from Phase A (TCMSP screening required). All 9 phases apply.
→ Partially characterised extract with some known actives — Screen unknowns via TCMSP; include confirmed compounds directly. Phases A (partial) through I.
→ Fully standardised with confirmed phytochemical markers — Skip TCMSP screening. Start from Phase B (target prediction). Report ADME for completeness only.
→ Pure isolated compound or approved drug — Skip Phases A entirely. Begin directly at Phase B with SMILES retrieval.
Q2 — What is your publication target?
→ Conference abstract (ongoing study) — Phases A–D sufficient. PPI network and docking stated as ongoing.
→ Q2 journal (e.g. International Wound Journal) — Phases A–G required. MD simulation optional.
→ Q1 journal (e.g. Phytomedicine IF 8.3) — All phases A–I required including MD simulation and wet lab concordance table.
Q3 — Do you have existing wet lab data?
→ Yes — extensive preclinical bioactivity data — Your strongest asset. Phase I concordance table becomes the centrepiece of the paper. Frame the study as "computational validation of experimental findings."
→ Yes — limited data — Use what you have for Phase I. Clearly distinguish predicted targets from experimentally confirmed ones.
→ No wet lab data — NP + docking study only. Clearly frame as hypothesis-generating. Molecular docking becomes your primary validation layer. Target Q2 journals.
Phase A
🌿
Compound Collection & ADME Filtering
When: required for unknown/uncharacterised extracts · optional for confirmed compounds
⚡ Skip rule: If your compounds are already experimentally confirmed (standardised extract with validated markers, isolated pure compounds, or approved drugs), skip the TCMSP screening step. Go directly to Step A3 (SMILES retrieval) and report ADME values in a supplementary table for convention only. State this clearly in your methods: "Compounds were included based on prior experimental characterisation. ADME properties are reported for completeness."
A1
Compound retrieval from herb databases
TCMSP · HERB database · Published literature
▼
  • 1Go to tcmsp-e.com and click "Herbs" in the navigation menu
  • 2Search each herb by scientific name (e.g. Cinnamomum zeylanicum). Try common names or related species if no result.
  • 3Click the herb name to open its ingredient list. Note the total number of compounds listed.
  • 4Apply ADME filters: set OB ≥ 30% and DL ≥ 0.18. Record all compounds that pass.
  • 5Supplement with HERB database (herb.ac.cn) for herbs with limited TCMSP coverage.
  • 6Add any compounds confirmed in published literature for that herb even if not in TCMSP.
  • 7Merge compound lists from all herbs. Remove duplicates. This is your active compound pool.
ParameterThresholdWhat it meansReference
Oral Bioavailability (OB)≥ 30%Minimum absorption from oral route to reach systemic circulationRu et al. 2014
Drug-likeness (DL)≥ 0.18Resemblance to known drug molecules based on Lipinski descriptorsRu et al. 2014
Lipinski MW≤ 500 DaMolecular weight — larger molecules have poor absorptionLipinski 2001
Lipinski LogP≤ 5Lipophilicity — determines membrane permeabilityLipinski 2001
tcmsp-e.com herb.ac.cn Ref: Ru et al. 2014 · Lipinski 2001
A2
ADME verification (optional)
SwissADME · pkCSM
▼
  • 1Go to swissadme.ch and paste SMILES of your compounds (one per line)
  • 2Run the analysis. Review the Lipinski, Ghose, Veber, Egan, and Muegge filters.
  • 3Record values for MW, LogP, HBD, HBA, TPSA. Include in Supplementary Table 1 of your paper.
  • 4For confirmed compounds: report these values but do not use them as inclusion criteria.
Output: Supplementary Table 1 — ADME properties of all study compounds. This table is expected by reviewers even when ADME screening was not used as a selection criterion.
swissadme.ch pkCSM online
A3
Retrieve SMILES strings & 3D structures
PubChem · ChemSpider
▼
  • 1Go to pubchem.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov. Search each compound by name.
  • 2On each compound page, copy the Canonical SMILES string from the "Structures" section.
  • 3Download the 3D SDF file (needed for molecular docking later). Save with compound name.
  • 4Record the CID number for each compound for citation in methods.
  • 5Build a master table: Compound name | CID | SMILES | MW | Formula.
SMILES explained: A SMILES string is the chemical structure written as text characters (e.g. Curcumin: COc1cc(/C=C/C(=O)...)). It is the input format used by SwissTargetPrediction and PharmMapper. You do not need to understand the structure — just copy it accurately.
pubchem.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov chemspider.com
Phase B
🎯
Molecular Target Prediction
When: always required · use minimum 2 tools for rigour
Why multiple tools? Each tool uses a different algorithm. SwissTargetPrediction uses structural similarity; PharmMapper uses 3D pharmacophore mapping. A target missed by one tool may be found by the other. Take the union of results from all tools — any target predicted by at least one tool is included.
B1
SwissTargetPrediction — structure-based prediction
Best for: polyphenols, flavonoids, known natural product classes
▼
  • 1Go to swisstargetprediction.ch
  • 2Paste SMILES of your first compound in the input box
  • 3Set organism to Homo sapiens — critical, do not leave as default
  • 4Click "Predict Targets" and wait 10–30 seconds for results
  • 5Click Download to save results as CSV. Name file: compoundname_SwissTP.csv
  • 6Repeat for all compounds. You will have one CSV per compound.
  • 7Note the "Probability" column — this is your confidence score for each prediction.
ScoreInterpretationUse in analysis
≥ 0.1High confidence prediction✓ Include as computational prediction in results
0.001–0.099Low confidenceInclude in network but do not emphasise in abstract
0.000Noise floor — not a real predictionDo NOT report as computational prediction. Only include if wet lab confirms.
⚠ Critical limitation: SwissTargetPrediction performs poorly for structurally unusual compounds (diterpene lactones like Andrographolide, complex terpenoids). If your key compound returns mostly probability = 0.000, supplement with PharmMapper.
swisstargetprediction.ch Ref: Daina et al. 2019 · Gfeller et al. 2014
B2
PharmMapper — pharmacophore-based prediction
Best for: structurally unusual compounds, terpenoids, alkaloids
▼
  • 1Go to pharmmapper.org
  • 2Upload the 3D SDF file of your compound (downloaded from PubChem in Step A3)
  • 3Set: Number of Reserved Matched Targets = 300, Select Human Protein Targets only
  • 4Submit. Processing takes 10–30 minutes. You will receive an email with a result link.
  • 5Download results. The key column is "Target Name" and "Fit Score".
  • 6Keep all targets — PharmMapper does not use a probability score like SwissTP. Take the full list.
  • 7PharmMapper returns protein names (not gene symbols) — these need UniProt mapping (Step B3).
pharmmapper.org Ref: Wang et al. 2017
B3
Standardise gene names — UniProt ID Mapping
Critical: must be done before merging tools
▼
  • 1Go to uniprot.org/id-mapping
  • 2Paste all protein names from PharmMapper output (one per line)
  • 3Set "From" = Protein Name · Set "To" = UniProtKB · Set Organism = Homo sapiens
  • 4Click Map. Download results in Excel format.
  • 5Use the Gene Names (primary) column as your standardised identifier going forward.
  • 6For unmapped entries: search manually in UniProt or exclude if human protein cannot be confirmed.
  • 7Note: SwissTargetPrediction already returns gene symbols — only PharmMapper output needs this step.
Why this matters: TNF in SwissTargetPrediction = "Tumor necrosis factor" in PharmMapper = P01375 in UniProt. All the same protein. Without standardisation, your overlap analysis misses matches due to naming inconsistency.
uniprot.org/id-mapping Ref: UniProt Consortium 2023
B4
Merge & deduplicate compound target list
Union of all prediction tools → one master list
▼
  • 1Combine all CSV files from SwissTargetPrediction + standardised PharmMapper results into one spreadsheet
  • 2Create columns: Gene Symbol | Compound 1 | Compound 2 | ... | Compound N | Count
  • 3Fill in probability scores (or ✓/✗) for each compound-target pair
  • 4Remove duplicate gene entries — each gene appears only once as a row
  • 5Calculate "Count" column = number of compounds predicting that target. Higher count = stronger candidate.
  • 6This is your Compound Target Master List — input for the Venn analysis in Phase D.
Output: Master compound target list (typically 300–600 unique gene symbols). Presented as Supplementary Table 2 in the paper.
Phase C
🏥
Disease Target Collection
When: always required · use all 3 databases · apply score thresholds
Common mistake to avoid: Do NOT use a "top 50" or "top 100" arbitrary gene count limit. This misses biologically relevant targets that rank lower due to less published literature rather than lower relevance. Always apply the published score threshold (GeneCards ≥ 20, DisGeNET ≥ 0.1) instead.
C1
GeneCards — disease gene collection
Use relevance score ≥ 20, not top-N cutoff
▼
  • 1Go to genecards.org
  • 2Search your disease term (e.g. "type 2 diabetes mellitus", "wound healing", "diabetic wound")
  • 3On results page, look for the GeneCards Relevance Score column. Filter or sort by score ≥ 20.
  • 4Run multiple search terms for each disease to maximise coverage. Record results per search.
  • 5Download gene list (or manually copy gene symbols). Record the relevance score for each.
  • 6Genes appearing in multiple searches = higher confidence disease association. Mark these.
Recommended search terms per disease:
T2DM: "type 2 diabetes mellitus" · "insulin resistance" · "hyperglycaemia" · "diabetes mellitus type 2"
Wound healing: "wound healing" · "diabetic wound" · "chronic wound" · "wound repair"
genecards.org Ref: Stelzer et al. 2016
C2
DisGeNET — gene-disease association database
Use GDA score ≥ 0.1 for high confidence associations
▼
  • 1Go to disgenet.org and click "Diseases" in the search bar
  • 2Search your disease by name or UMLS ID (T2DM: C0011860 · Wound healing: C0043240)
  • 3Click the disease to open its gene association list
  • 4Filter by Score GDA ≥ 0.1. This is the published minimum threshold for high-confidence associations.
  • 5Download the gene list. The key columns are Gene Symbol and Score GDA.
  • 6Genes with Score GDA ≥ 0.5 are especially strong — note these as high-confidence disease genes.
GDA ScoreEvidence levelMeaning
0.9–1.0Very strongMultiple curated databases + many publications + animal models
0.5–0.9StrongCurated sources + good publication support
0.1–0.5ModerateSome evidence — acceptable for network pharmacology
< 0.1WeakInsufficient evidence — exclude
disgenet.org Ref: Piñero et al. 2020
C3
OMIM — confirmed Mendelian disease genes
High-confidence anchor genes — include all results
▼
  • 1Go to omim.org and search your disease
  • 2Click the disease entry. Scroll to "Gene-Phenotype Relationships" section.
  • 3Record all genes listed. OMIM only contains confirmed causal gene-disease relationships — no score filter needed.
  • 4These genes serve as high-confidence anchors in your disease gene set.
omim.org Ref: Amberger et al. 2019
C4
Merge all disease gene lists
Union of GeneCards + DisGeNET + OMIM → master disease gene set
▼
  • 1Combine gene lists from GeneCards, DisGeNET, and OMIM into one spreadsheet
  • 2Add a source column indicating which database(s) each gene came from
  • 3Remove duplicates — each gene appears only once
  • 4Genes from multiple databases = higher confidence. Add a "database count" column.
  • 5This is your Master Disease Gene Set — input for the Venn analysis.
Output: Master disease gene set (typically 200–800 genes for a well-characterised disease). Report total gene count per database in your methods section.
Phase D
⭕
Venn Overlap Analysis — Candidate Target Identification
When: always required · this is the scientific core of the study
D1
Venn intersection: compound targets ∩ disease genes
Genes in BOTH lists = candidate therapeutic targets
▼
  • 1Take your Compound Target Master List (Phase B output) and Disease Gene Set (Phase C output)
  • 2Find genes that appear in BOTH lists. This intersection = your candidate therapeutic targets.
  • 3Use jvenn.fr or Venny 2.1 online to generate a Venn diagram automatically
  • 4For Python users: use the set.intersection() method or pandas merge
  • 5Record: total compound targets | total disease genes | overlap count | Venn diagram
  • 6The Venn diagram becomes Figure 1 of your paper
Typical overlap rates: For a well-characterised disease, expect 5–15% overlap between compound targets and disease genes. Too low = your compounds may not be disease-relevant. Too high = your disease gene set may be too broad.
jvenn.fr Venny 2.1 online Python / R
D2
Build candidate target tally table
Multi-compound targets are your strongest candidates
▼
  • 1Create a table: rows = overlapping genes, columns = each compound
  • 2Fill in probability scores (or ✓/✗) for each compound-target pair
  • 3Add a "compound count" column. Targets predicted by more compounds = prioritise for hub analysis and docking.
  • 4Add a "wet lab confirmed" column — mark any target already confirmed by existing experimental data
  • 5Sort by compound count descending. Top 20 targets = your primary candidates for Phase E.
Output: Candidate target tally table (typically 20–100 genes). This becomes Supplementary Table 3. The top section of this table drives the Discussion narrative.
Phase E
🕸
PPI Network Construction & Hub Target Identification
When: required for journal paper · not needed for conference abstract only
E1
Build PPI network in STRING
Maps how candidate targets interact with each other
▼
  • 1Go to string-db.org → click "Multiple Proteins" search
  • 2Paste your overlapping gene list (from Phase D). Set organism = Homo sapiens.
  • 3Set Confidence score ≥ 0.7 (high confidence). This removes weak/speculative interactions.
  • 4Click "Continue". View the resulting network on screen.
  • 5Go to Exports → Download the TSV network file for Cytoscape import.
  • 6Also download the "Node attributes" file — contains degree scores pre-calculated by STRING.
Confidence settingScoreWhen to use
High confidence≥ 0.7Standard for NP papers — recommended
Medium confidence≥ 0.4For small gene sets where high confidence produces too sparse a network
Highest confidence≥ 0.9Only for very well-studied diseases with extensive interaction data
string-db.org Ref: Szklarczyk et al. 2023
E2
Hub target identification in Cytoscape
CytoHubba plugin · degree centrality
▼
  • 1Open Cytoscape (free desktop software, cytoscape.org). Install CytoHubba plugin via Apps menu.
  • 2Import STRING network: File → Import → Network from File → select your TSV file
  • 3In Apps → CytoHubba → select Degree method → Submit
  • 4CytoHubba ranks all nodes by degree. Top 10 nodes = hub targets — your molecular docking candidates.
  • 5For additional topological validation, also run: Betweenness Centrality (BC) and Closeness Centrality (CC) via CytoNCA plugin.
  • 6Hub targets = nodes with high Degree + high BC + high CC. Export this ranked list.
  • 7Style the network: node size proportional to degree, edge thickness proportional to confidence. Export as Figure 2.
What is a hub target? A protein that connects to many others in the network. In biology, hub proteins are regulatory nodes — targeting them produces broad downstream effects. In NP papers, hub targets are reported as the primary therapeutic targets of the formulation.
Cytoscape CytoHubba plugin CytoNCA plugin Ref: Shannon et al. 2003
E3
Herb-Compound-Target (H-C-T) network
Visualises polypharmacology — standard Figure 2 in NP papers
▼
  • 1Build a network in Cytoscape with three node types: Herb → Compound → Target (from your tally table)
  • 2Edge = relationship (herb contains compound; compound predicts target)
  • 3Calculate degree centrality. Nodes with high degree = key compounds or targets with broad connections.
  • 4Style: different shapes for herbs/compounds/targets. Node size = degree. Export as Figure 2.
  • 5This figure visually demonstrates polypharmacology — the same target being hit by multiple compounds from different herbs.
Phase F
📊
GO & KEGG Pathway Enrichment Analysis
When: required for journal paper · DAVID is the tool; GO and KEGG are the analyses
F1
GO & KEGG enrichment via DAVID
DAVID is the platform; GO and KEGG are the two analyses you run within it
▼
  • 1Go to david.ncifcrf.gov → click "Start Analysis"
  • 2Paste your hub target gene list (from Phase E) in the Gene List input box
  • 3Select Identifier = OFFICIAL_GENE_SYMBOL, List Type = Gene List
  • 4Set Species = Homo sapiens. Click Submit List.
  • 5In the left panel, under Gene Ontology, select: GOTERM_BP_DIRECT (Biological Process)
  • 6Under Pathways, select: KEGG_PATHWAY
  • 7Click Functional Annotation Chart. Download results.
  • 8Filter results: keep only terms with p < 0.05 (Benjamini corrected) and gene count ≥ 2
  • 9Visualise as a bubble chart (x = gene ratio, y = pathway name, bubble size = gene count, colour = p-value). This is Figure 3.
Analysis typeWhat it tells youSignificance threshold
GO Biological ProcessWhat biological processes your targets are involved inp < 0.05 BH corrected

KEGG pathways to look for in T2DM/wound healing studies: PI3K-AKT · AGE-RAGE · TNF signalling · TGF-β · NF-κB · MAPK · HIF-1 · VEGF · PPAR · Insulin resistance · ECM-receptor interaction

Phase G
🔬
Molecular Docking
When: required for journal paper · the structural validation layer
G1
Protein (receptor) preparation
Download PDB structure → clean → prepare for docking
▼
  • 1Go to rcsb.org. Search for your hub target protein (e.g. "MMP1 Homo sapiens"). Select a high-resolution crystal structure (< 2.5 Å preferred).
  • 2Download the PDB file. Note the PDB ID for methods citation (e.g. 1SU3).
  • 3Open in AutoDockTools or PyMOL. Remove water molecules (HOH), co-crystallised ligands, and non-standard residues.
  • 4Add polar hydrogen atoms. Assign Gasteiger partial charges.
  • 5Define the binding pocket (grid box) centred on the co-crystallised ligand position or known active site residues.
  • 6Save as PDBQT format for AutoDock Vina input.
rcsb.org AutoDockTools PyMOL
G2
Ligand (compound) preparation
Energy minimise 3D structure → convert to docking format
▼
  • 1Retrieve 3D SDF file from PubChem (downloaded in Step A3) for each marker compound
  • 2Energy minimise using Avogadro (free) or Open Babel: force field MMFF94, 500 steps
  • 3Add hydrogens. Assign Gasteiger charges.
  • 4Convert to PDBQT format using Open Babel or AutoDockTools
  • 5Always include reference/control drugs — dock the same approved drug that you compare against in wet lab. This allows direct computational-experimental comparison.
Avogadro Open Babel AutoDockTools
G3
Run molecular docking — AutoDock Vina
Calculate binding affinity for each compound-target pair
▼
  • 1Open PyRx (GUI wrapper for AutoDock Vina — easier for beginners). Import protein and ligand files.
  • 2Set grid box dimensions centred on binding pocket (typically 20×20×20 Å for drug-like molecules)
  • 3Set exhaustiveness = 8 (standard). Higher = more accurate but slower.
  • 4Run docking. Record the lowest binding energy (most negative kcal/mol) for each compound-target pair.
  • 5Build a results table: rows = targets, columns = each compound + reference drug. Report binding energy in kcal/mol.
  • 6Validate: re-dock the co-crystallised ligand and confirm RMSD < 2.0 Å — this confirms your protocol is accurate.
Binding energyInterpretation
≤ −7.0 kcal/molStrong binding — excellent candidate. Better than or comparable to many reference drugs.
−5.0 to −7.0Moderate binding — acceptable. Report and discuss.
≥ −5.0 kcal/molWeak binding — report but de-emphasise. May still be relevant in combination.
Key comparison: If your compound's binding energy is more negative than the reference drug against the same target, that is a strong result. If wet lab IC₅₀ also shows your compound is more potent than the reference drug — that is a three-way convergence (wet lab + computational structure + binding energy).
AutoDock Vina PyRx (GUI) Ref: Trott & Olson 2010
G4
Visualise binding interactions
PyMOL / Discovery Studio → binding pose + interaction map
▼
  • 1Open docking output in PyMOL or Discovery Studio Visualizer (both free)
  • 2Identify hydrogen bonds, hydrophobic contacts, and key amino acid residues interacting with the compound
  • 3Generate 2D interaction diagrams in Discovery Studio (Analyze → Ligand Interactions)
  • 4Compare binding pose of your compound against the reference drug at the same target
  • 5Export publication-quality images for Figure 4. Include both 3D pose and 2D interaction map.
PyMOL Discovery Studio Visualizer UCSF Chimera
Phase H — Optional
⚡
Molecular Dynamics (MD) Simulation
When: optional but recommended for Q1 journals · top 2 docking hits only
The Q1 differentiator: MD simulation confirms that your docking results are stable over time, not just a snapshot binding pose. Reviewers at Phytomedicine, Journal of Ethnopharmacology, and Biomedicine & Pharmacotherapy increasingly expect this. Run only for your top 1–2 compound-target pairs. A 100 ns GROMACS simulation takes approximately 12–48 hours on a standard workstation.
H1
MD simulation protocol — GROMACS
100 ns · CHARMM36 force field · RMSD + RMSF + MM-GBSA
▼
  • 1Select top 2 compound-target pairs from docking (most negative binding energy + wet lab confirmation)
  • 2Prepare the protein-ligand complex from your docking output in GROMACS format
  • 3Solvate in TIP3P water box, add Na⁺/Cl⁻ ions to neutralise
  • 4Energy minimise → NVT equilibration (300 K, 100 ps) → NPT equilibration (1 bar, 100 ps) → Production MD (100 ns)
  • 5Analyse: RMSD (structural stability over time), RMSF (residue flexibility), H-bond count (interaction stability)
  • 6Calculate MM-GBSA binding free energy — more accurate than docking score alone
  • 7Stable RMSD (< 2 Å fluctuation) + maintained H-bonds + negative MM-GBSA = confirmed stable binding
AnalysisAcceptable resultWhat it means
RMSD< 2.0 Å plateauSystem is stable — complex does not fall apart over 100 ns
RMSFLow at active site residuesBinding pocket is rigid — compound is well-anchored
MM-GBSA< −30 kcal/molFavourable and stable binding free energy
GROMACS AMBER (alternative) CHARMM36 force field
Phase I
✅
Wet Lab Validation & Concordance Integration
When: whenever existing bioactivity data is available · the paper's strongest differentiator
Why this phase is your competitive advantage: Most published NP studies are purely computational with no experimental validation beyond docking. Any existing wet lab data — IC₅₀ values, protein expression, cell assays, in vivo results — that aligns with your NP predictions transforms the paper from hypothesis-generating to hypothesis-confirming. This is the difference between a Q2 and Q1 acceptance.
I1
Build the wet lab concordance table
The centrepiece of the Discussion section
▼
  • 1For each computationally predicted target, check whether existing bioactivity data confirms activity at that target or along that pathway
  • 2Build a table with columns: Target | Wet lab evidence | Experimental value | Compound(s) driving it (computational) | Predicted target (computational) | Agreement
  • 3Each row where computational prediction + wet lab agree = a concordant hit. Report the total count prominently.
  • 4For targets with wet lab data but no computational prediction: report as orthogonal evidence. Discuss as a limitation of the prediction algorithm.
  • 5This table becomes Table 4 of the paper (or Figure 5 if presented as a graphical concordance map)
The perfect concordance statement reads: "Network pharmacology predicted X, Y, Z as hub targets. This is independently corroborated by measured IC₅₀ of A µg/mL against target X, upregulation of Y protein expression by B%, and inhibition of Z by C% — establishing a two-independent-method confirmation for each key mechanism."
